Lesson 01 · Charge, current, voltage, and power

01 · The quantities, briefly through water, then on their own

Charge (\(Q\), coulombs) is the fundamental quantity; current (\(I\), amperes) is its rate of flow, \(I=dQ/dt\). Voltage (\(V\), volts) is the electrical potential difference that drives current between two points. A water analogy — charge as water volume, current as flow rate, voltage as pressure difference — gives a useful first mental picture, but it breaks down quickly for AC, for reactive elements, and for anything beyond the simplest DC circuit. It is used once, here, and retired; from this point the course works directly with the real electrical quantities.

02 · Ohm's law and power dissipation

Ohm's law, \(V=IR\), relates voltage across a resistive element to the current through it, with resistance \(R\) (ohms) as the constant of proportionality. Power dissipated in a resistance can be written three equivalent ways — \( P=VI=I^2R=V^2/R \) — pick whichever form uses the two quantities already known. The \(I^2R\) form matters most for a loose or corroded connection: it is simply an extra resistance in series with the rest of the circuit, carrying the same current as everything else on that path. Because power there scales with the square of current, doubling the load current quadruples the heat generated at that one weak point, not merely doubles it — the direct reason a connection that runs only warm at light load can become a genuine fire risk once demand rises, and why torque-checking terminal connections is real maintenance, not a formality.

03 · Reading a multimeter without folklore

A meter set to measure voltage is connected in parallel across the two points of interest and must present very high input impedance, ideally infinite, so it draws negligible current and does not disturb the circuit or load down a sensitive source. A meter set to measure current is connected in series, breaking the circuit to insert itself, and must present very low input impedance, ideally zero, so it adds no meaningful resistance to the path. The classic, entirely avoidable mistake is leaving a meter in current mode — low internal impedance — and then connecting it in parallel across a voltage source: this creates a near short-circuit through the meter's low internal resistance, drawing a large current that can blow its fuse or damage it outright. Input impedance, matched to the mode setting, is exactly what protects against this in both directions.

04 · Working method for a basic circuit reading

(1) Identify what is actually being asked — voltage, a difference between two points measured in parallel, or current, a flow through one path measured in series. (2) Set the meter to the matching mode, which sets its internal impedance to match. (3) Apply \(V=IR\), or one of its power forms, using whichever two of \(V\), \(I\), \(R\), \(P\) are already known to find what is missing. This three-step sequence, more than any single formula, is the actual skill this lesson builds.

Key relation — power dissipation

\[ P = I^2R \]

Quadratic in current — the reason a loose connection is genuinely dangerous, not just inefficient.

Lesson 02 · DC circuit analysis

01 · Series-parallel reduction

Series resistors carry the same current, and their voltages add: \( R_{series}=R_1+R_2+\dots \) — always larger than the largest individual resistor. Parallel resistors share the same voltage, and their currents add: \( 1/R_{parallel}=1/R_1+1/R_2+\dots \) — always smaller than the smallest individual resistor, because every added parallel branch gives current an additional path, so total resistance to current flow can only fall, never rise, as a branch is added. The working method for any network: redraw it repeatedly, combining series and parallel groups from whichever end is simplest, until a single equivalent resistance remains.

02 · Kirchhoff's laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL): the sum of currents into any node equals the sum of currents out — charge does not accumulate at a node, so whatever flows in must flow out. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the sum of voltage rises and drops around any closed loop equals zero. Physically, KVL conserves energy: starting and returning to the same point means the net potential change must be zero regardless of the path taken around the loop — the same underlying idea that makes a state property path-independent in an entirely different course's energy balances.

03 · Voltage and current dividers

A voltage divider (series resistors) gives the voltage across any one resistor as the source voltage times that resistor's share of the total series resistance: \( V_{R2}=V_{source}\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \). A current divider (two parallel resistors) gives the current through one branch as \( I_{R1}=I_{total}\times\dfrac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \) — note the other resistor appears in the numerator, since more current always takes the path of less resistance, so the fraction going through \(R_1\) depends on \(R_2\)'s size, not its own.

04 · Practical sources and battery-fed control circuits

A real (non-ideal) voltage source carries an internal resistance \(r_{int}\) in series with an ideal source, so its terminal voltage sags under load: \( V_{terminal}=V_{source}-I\,r_{int} \). A battery reads its full open-circuit voltage with nothing connected, but sags noticeably once a real load — a starter motor, a control relay coil — draws current. A weak or aged battery, with a higher \(r_{int}\), can show an entirely acceptable open-circuit voltage yet still fail to crank a real load, because the sag under actual current draw, not the resting voltage, is what determines whether the load actually runs.

Key rule — series and parallel

Series result is always larger than any part. Parallel result is always smaller than any part. Together, this is the entire reduction method.

Lesson 03 · Capacitance and inductance

01 · Energy storage in fields

A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between two conductive plates; its current-voltage relation is \( I=C\,dV/dt \) — current depends on how fast voltage is changing, not on the voltage itself, so a capacitor passes no current once fully charged at steady DC (\(dV/dt=0\)). An inductor stores energy in a magnetic field built by current flowing through a coil; its relation is \( V=L\,dI/dt \) — voltage depends on how fast current is changing, so an inductor looks like a plain wire, zero voltage drop, once its current is no longer changing at steady DC.

02 · First-order transients and time constants

An RC circuit's voltage or current approaches its final value exponentially with time constant \( \tau=RC \); an RL circuit's current does the same with \( \tau=L/R \). After one \(\tau\), a transient is roughly 63% complete; after about five \(\tau\), it is essentially settled. For a contactor coil specifically, \( \tau=L/R \) sets how quickly coil current — and therefore magnetic pull-in force — builds after the coil is energized; a coil with a larger \(\tau\) relative to its resistance takes measurably longer to pull in, a detail that matters for control-timing and for diagnosing a sluggish contactor.

03 · Why inductive loads arc switches

An inductor resists sudden changes in current: \(V=L\,dI/dt\) means a very fast \(di/dt\) implies, in principle, an unboundedly large voltage. Opening a switch or contact carrying current through an inductive load — a motor coil, a contactor coil, a solenoid — tries to force current from some value to zero almost instantly. The inductor responds by generating a large voltage spike across the opening contact gap, large enough to ionize the air and draw an arc, which continues carrying current — and eroding the contact surface — until the stored magnetic energy is fully dissipated. Suppression — a flyback diode across a DC coil, or an RC snubber across an AC contact — gives that current a controlled alternate path to decay through smoothly instead of being forced to an abrupt stop, keeping \(di/dt\), and the induced voltage, bounded.

04 · Working method

(1) Identify whether an element stores energy in an electric field (capacitor, current depends on \(dV/dt\)) or a magnetic field (inductor, voltage depends on \(dI/dt\)). (2) For a transient, identify \(R\) and either \(C\) or \(L\) to compute the time constant. (3) For a switching or interruption problem, recognize that any element carrying current through an inductance needs a path for that current to decay smoothly — or expect an arc.

Key relation — the inductive arc

\[ V = L\,\frac{dI}{dt} \]

Forcing di/dt large forces V large — the reason interrupting an inductive circuit arcs, and why suppression works.

Lesson 04 · AC fundamentals and phasors

01 · Sinusoids and RMS values

An AC quantity \(v(t)=V_{max}\sin(\omega t)\) constantly changes — its peak value alone overstates its actual heating or working effect, while its average over a full cycle is exactly zero. RMS (root-mean-square) is defined so that an RMS AC value produces the same heating effect — the same average power in a resistor — as an equal DC value. For a pure sinusoid, \( V_{rms}=V_{max}/\sqrt2\approx0.707\,V_{max} \). This is why "230 V AC" is an RMS rating, not a peak — the honest, working-equivalent number, with the actual peak running about \(\sqrt2\) times higher, near 325 V.

02 · Phasor representation

A phasor is a rotating-vector shorthand for a sinusoid at one fixed frequency: instead of tracking \(v(t)\) through time, a phasor captures a sinusoid's magnitude and phase angle as a single vector or complex number. This turns the calculus of sinusoids — derivatives and integrals — into simple vector or complex algebra, a simplification that works because every quantity in a linear AC circuit at one frequency is itself a sinusoid at that same frequency, differing only in magnitude and phase.

03 · Impedance of R, L, C

Impedance \(Z\) generalizes resistance to AC. A resistor's impedance is simply \(Z_R=R\), no phase shift. An inductor's impedance, \(Z_L=j\omega L\), grows with frequency and shifts voltage \(90^\circ\) ahead of current. A capacitor's impedance, \(Z_C=1/(j\omega C)\), shrinks with frequency and shifts the opposite way — current ahead of voltage. Impedance adds to resistance exactly the concept resistance alone in a DC circuit never needed: a phase relationship between voltage and current, not just a magnitude ratio. Series impedances combine the same way resistances do, \(Z_{total}=Z_1+Z_2+\dots\), but as complex numbers, so magnitudes generally do not simply add unless the phase angles happen to match.

04 · The 50 Hz world, quantified

At \(f=50\ \text{Hz}\) (or 60 Hz in some countries), \( \omega=2\pi f\approx314\ \text{rad/s} \) — this single number turns \(L\) (henries) and \(C\) (farads) into actual ohms of impedance at mains frequency: \(Z_L=\omega L\), \(Z_C=1/(\omega C)\). Working method: (1) convert every element to its impedance at the operating frequency; (2) combine impedances exactly as resistances combine — series adds, parallel via reciprocals — but with complex arithmetic; (3) apply the AC form of Ohm's law, \(V=IZ\), using RMS magnitudes and phase angles as needed.

Key rating — RMS

\[ V_{rms} = \frac{V_{max}}{\sqrt2} \]

The honest, heating-equivalent rating for a sinusoid — not the peak.

Lesson 05 · AC power and power factor

01 · Real, reactive, and apparent power

Real power \(P\) (watts, kW) is what is actually converted to useful work or heat — a motor's shaft output, a heater's element. Reactive power \(Q\) (VAR, kVAR) oscillates back and forth between the source and any inductive or capacitive element, doing no net useful work but still requiring current to flow to sustain the magnetic or electric field involved. Apparent power \(S\) (VA, kVA) is simply \(S=VI\) using RMS values — what the supply, the wiring, the transformer, the generator, must actually be sized to carry, since it must carry the current whether or not that current does useful work. The three relate as a right triangle, \(S^2=P^2+Q^2\), with the angle \(\varphi\) between \(P\) and \(S\) equal to the angle by which current lags or leads voltage.

02 · Power factor — why utilities bill for it

Power factor, \( PF=P/S=\cos\varphi \), is the fraction of apparent power actually doing useful work. A plant at \(PF=0.7\) draws \(S=P/0.7\approx1.43P\) in apparent power for the same real power — the wiring, transformers, and generation capacity serving that plant must all be sized roughly 43% larger than the real power alone would require, purely to carry reactive current that does no useful work. Utilities bill for poor power factor, directly through a penalty or indirectly through a demand/kVA charge, because that extra capacity is real infrastructure cost they bear whether or not it ever converts to useful output for the customer.

03 · Correction capacitors, sized

Most industrial reactive power is inductive — motors, transformers, ballasts — a lagging power factor. A capacitor bank connected locally supplies leading reactive power that cancels some or all of the inductive reactive power the load demands from the utility; the load still draws its full original reactive current, but in a short local loop with the capacitor rather than all the way back through the utility's own wiring and transformers. The reactive power the utility actually sees becomes \( Q_{new}=Q_{old}-Q_{capacitor} \); sizing a correction bank means computing the reactive-power reduction needed to reach a target power factor and selecting a capacitor rated for that many kVAR.

04 · Working method

(1) Find \(P\), the real power, from the load's actual work output or a direct measurement. (2) Find \(Q\) or \(S\) from measurement, or from the load's known power factor. (3) Apply \(S^2=P^2+Q^2\) for whichever of the three is still missing. (4) If correction is needed, compute the target \(Q\) at the desired power factor, and the capacitor kVAR required to close the gap between the actual \(Q\) and that target.

Key relations — power triangle

\[ S^2=P^2+Q^2,\qquad PF=\frac{P}{S}=\cos\varphi. \]

Lesson 06 · Three-phase systems

01 · Wye and delta connections

A wye (star) connection joins three windings or loads at a common neutral point, with each winding's other end brought out as a line — this gives access to both line-to-line and line-to-neutral voltages. A delta connection joins the three windings end-to-end in a closed loop, with each junction brought out as a line — there is no neutral point, and only line-to-line voltage exists. Line quantities, measured on the supply conductors, and phase quantities, measured across one winding or load element, are not automatically the same — the relationship between them depends entirely on which connection is used.

02 · Line vs. phase relationships

In a wye connection: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \) — line voltage is about 1.732 times the phase voltage — while \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \), since line and winding share the same series path. The \(\sqrt3\) factor comes directly from the \(120^\circ\) phasor geometry: the vector difference between two phase voltages \(120^\circ\) apart has magnitude \(\sqrt3\) times either one's magnitude, not simply double. In a delta connection, it reverses: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), since each line connects directly across one winding, while \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \), since each line current is now the phasor difference of two phase currents.

03 · Three-phase power — constant delivery

Three-phase real power is given compactly by \( P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF \), using line quantities directly regardless of whether the system is wye or delta — exactly why this is the practical field formula. A single-phase voltage and current both cross zero twice per cycle, so their instantaneous product — power — also dips to zero twice per cycle: single-phase power delivery pulses. With three phases spaced \(120^\circ\) apart, each phase's instantaneous power still pulses individually, but the three pulses stagger exactly enough that their sum is mathematically constant for a balanced load — the real reason three-phase motors run smoother than single-phase ones, with no torque pulsation traceable to the electrical supply itself.

04 · Why industrial plants are three-phase places

Three-phase delivers more power per conductor weight than an equivalent single-phase system, provides constant, non-pulsating power delivery to motors — smoother torque, less vibration, less mechanical stress — and is what allows a simple, cheap rotating magnetic field, the subject of Lesson 8, to be generated directly from the supply with no extra starting circuitry. Working method: (1) determine whether the connection is wye or delta; (2) convert between line and phase quantities using \(\sqrt3\) in the correct direction for that connection; (3) apply \(P=\sqrt3\,V_{line}I_{line}\,PF\) for total three-phase real power.

Key ratios — wye vs. delta

Wye: \( V_{line}=\sqrt3\,V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=I_{phase} \). Delta: \( V_{line}=V_{phase} \), \( I_{line}=\sqrt3\,I_{phase} \).
