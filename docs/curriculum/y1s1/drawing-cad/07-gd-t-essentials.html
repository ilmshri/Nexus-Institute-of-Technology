GD&T essentials

Feature control frames, datums, flatness, position, runout. What GD&T buys over coordinate tolerances. Runout callouts as the language of rotating-equipment repair standards.

Lecture

After this lesson you can answer:
  • What does total runout control that circular runout does not?
  • Why does GD&T's datum system beat coordinate tolerancing for function?

§1 · Why coordinates aren't enough

Plus-minus tolerances on coordinates control a hole's X and Y separately — but function cares about the hole's position as a whole, its axis attitude, the flatness of the face it lives on. Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing (GD&T) tolerances geometry directly: form (flatness, straightness), orientation (perpendicularity, parallelism), location (position, concentricity-family), and runout. Each control is a feature-control frame: symbol, tolerance zone, and the datums it's measured from.

§2 · Datums — the measurement contract

A datum reference frame (A|B|C) declares the exact sequence of surfaces the part sits on during measurement: primary A arrests three degrees of freedom, secondary B two, tertiary C one. The same part, gauged from different datums, yields different verdicts — so the drawing fixes the frame once, and design, manufacture, and inspection all argue inside it. Datums are chosen from function: the faces that seat, the bores that locate.

§3 · Runout — the rotating machine's own control

Circular runout limits the wobble of each circular element as the part turns on its datum axis — one dial reading per section. Total runout sweeps the whole surface axially and radially at once, controlling taper and coning that circular runout cannot see. For couplings, sleeves, and seal faces, runout tolerances are the drawing's way of promising your dial indicator a quiet needle — and Lesson 6's fits plus today's runout are the complete language of a shaft drawing.

Closing. GD&T tolerances what function feels — form, orientation, location, runout — from declared datums. Read the frame, find the datums, and the drawing tells you exactly how the inspector will set the part up.

Foundations

What this lesson assumes

  • Limits and fits (Lesson 6) — GD&T rides on toleranced features.
  • Datum thinking (Lesson 5) — formalized here into A|B|C frames.

Glossary

  • Feature-control frame — the boxed callout: symbol | zone | datums.
  • Datum reference frame — the seating sequence that arrests the part for measurement.
  • Position tolerance — a zone (usually ⌀) the feature's axis must live in.
  • MMC — maximum material condition; enables bonus tolerance and functional gauging.
  • Circular vs total runout — per-section wobble vs whole-surface sweep.

Worked Examples

Worked example 1 — position beats coordinates

Problem. A hole is located X = 50±0.1, Y = 50±0.1. Find: the worst-case radial position error, and the equivalent GD&T callout that controls it directly.

Solution. (1) Worst case: both coordinates off by 0.1 → radial error \( \sqrt{0.1^2 + 0.1^2} = 0.141 \) mm; the coordinate scheme actually permits a square zone whose corners stick out 41% farther than its sides. (2) GD&T equivalent: basic dimensions 50, 50 with position ⌀0.28 (A|B|C) — a round zone of the same worst-case radius, uniform in all directions. (3) Bonus: add Ⓜ and the zone grows as the hole departs MMC — functional gauging becomes possible.

Answer: ±0.1 coordinates permit 0.141 radial error; position ⌀0.28 controls the same function honestly. Sanity check: the diagonal of a square vs its side — the 41% is pure Pythagoras.

Worked example 2 — circular passes, total fails

Problem. A seal sleeve shows circular runout 0.02 at every measured section, yet total runout measures 0.09. Find: what geometry explains this, and which control the seal cares about.

Solution. (1) Each section is individually round about the axis (0.02) but the sections don't line up: the surface is tapered or coned — radius drifts along the length. (2) Total runout sweeps axially and accumulates that drift (0.09). (3) A lip seal riding that sleeve sees a changing interference along its width — total runout is the functional control for seal tracks and coupling rims.

Answer: taper/coning; specify total runout for seal surfaces. Sanity check: circular runout at many sections plus a straightness check ≈ total runout — the 0.07 gap is the taper the single-section dial could never see.

Kuwait Floor

Vignette — the coupling that "passed" and still shook. You arrive at a pump train that vibrates at 1× despite laser alignment certificates. The coupling hub's rim runout was checked — circular, mid-face, 0.02, pass. Swept across the full rim face, total runout comes in at 0.11: the hub face is coned from a previous overheat, and the alignment laser has been faithfully aligning to a lie. The hub is refaced; the 1× drops by two-thirds. Your close-out adds one line to the site's alignment procedure: face runout means total, swept, per the drawing's GD&T — not one ring at one radius.
